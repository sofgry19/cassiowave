Foundations of a Flourishing Society: A Joyful Exploration of Human Interaction

Student Name Sofia Grytsenko
TagsDesign ResearchHuman-Centered DesignInteraction DesignProduct InnovationProducts of DesignService DesignSocial Impact DesignSystems DesignUser Experience (UX) Design

My MFA thesis explores how design influences everyday emotions and experiences and how that influence can be used positively.

Design can create small, joyful moments in everyday urban life: especially for young adults moving through public spaces like streets, subways, and parks.

Through interviews, participatory workshops, and playful prototypes, I studied how objects, environments, and interactions can tap into people‚Äôs ‚Äúinner child,‚Äù support emotional well-being, and foster tiny bursts of connection in otherwise routine or stressful city days.

This lead to my theory: The Pillars of Joy. There are many products and experiences that bring people joy, but what are the factors that actually cause people to stop and appreciate the moment?

In order for joy to be long-lasting and impactful, it must include a sensory feature, spontaneous moment, and a shared connection with another soul. Only once these three factors are fulfilled does an experience or product truly effect one‚Äôs self.

Thesis Narrative

  • Toggle to read

    December 2025

    This thesis explores how small, tangible, emotionally honest interventions in public and commuting spaces can create brief moments of joy for stressed urban residents, particularly young adults in New York City. In a city where about 13% of adults‚Äîroughly 845,000 people‚Äîlive with depression and nearly one in four experience a mental health disorder in a given year, everyday mental strain is not abstract; it shapes how people move, relate, and protect themselves in public space. Long commutes, crowding, overwork, and social comparison leave many New Yorkers feeling rushed, guarded, and emotionally numb rather than connected or alive.
    Drawing on Positive Design, this work treats joy as one contributor to human flourishing, understood as people developing as individuals, living to their fullest potential, and acting in the best interests of society. Joy here is distinct from happiness. Happiness is longer-term life satisfaction and meaning; joy is brief and often embodied: a 30‚Äësecond softening when something breaks routine, feels emotionally safe, and is often shared or co‚Äëwitnessed with others. In Ingrid Fetell Lee‚Äôs work on ‚Äúaesthetics of joy,‚Äù playful forms, color, abundance, and nature reliably trigger this kind of momentary uplift. Barbara Ehrenreich‚Äôs history of collective joy shows how shared, synchronized experiences can bind people into temporary communities. Together, these sources suggest that joy is not frivolous; it is a social and spatial phenomenon with real implications for well‚Äëbeing.
    To ground these ideas, the project combines secondary research with qualitative work: interviews with architects, designers, and creative practitioners; observational visits to places like PopMart, Hekate cafe, and Storm King; and participatory studies such as ‚ÄúBalloon Day‚Äù and the ‚ÄúRoutes to Joy‚Äù workshop. Foundational insights from experts highlight three recurring themes: joy is closely tied to our ‚Äúinner child‚Äù and states of playful discovery; emotional safety and comfort zones enable positive risk‚Äëtaking; and encounters with nature, light, and good proportion soften people and make them more present. Participatory research with New Yorkers adds further nuance: deviations from routine create aliveness, joy multiplies when witnessed, creatures and creature‚Äëlike forms disarm people and invite connection, sensory features speak before the mind catches up, and even shared frustration can build community when acknowledged together.
    From this, the thesis proposes a simple theory of joy in public space: spontaneous moment + sensory feature + collaboration or co‚Äëwitnessing = joyful experience. Over time, joyful experiences can feed what this project calls a ‚Äúsociety wheel‚Äù: joyful experience ‚Üí positive emotions ‚Üí collaboration and communication with others ‚Üí joyful experiences for others. The design focus becomes clear: How might small, non‚Äëcommercial, emotionally honest interventions in commuting spaces gently disrupt autopilot, feel safe and human‚Äëmade, and invite shared noticing rather than performance?
